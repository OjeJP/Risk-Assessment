Introduction

Course objectives & methodology

The goal of this coursework was to select and implement risk identification and assessment methods for the fictional company JOP Corp — an imaginary flight school with significant technological operations. The five tasks were: identify and assess assets; identify and assess vulnerabilities; identify and assess threats; identify and assess risks and residual risks; and make recommendations for top management for the protection of IT security.

Five-phase methodology
PHASE 1 Assets identification and assessment ▶
Goal: Define the list of assets for JOP Corp and perform identification and assessment procedures.

Tasks:
  • Select all applicable types of assets for JOP Corp
  • Perform identification of assets in accordance with ISO 27001 / ISO 31000
  • Create a list of recommendations for top management about IT security protection for assets

Method: Assets assessed using two cost evaluation methods — Qualitative (Very Big, Big, Medium, Small, Very Small / High, Medium, Low) and Quantitative (5,4,3,2,1). Asset mark threshold set at 5 for escalation.
PHASE 2 Vulnerabilities identification and assessment ▶
Goal: Define the list of vulnerabilities for JOP Corp assets and perform assessment procedures.

Tasks:
  • Select all applicable vulnerability types for assets of JOP Corp
  • Perform assessment of vulnerabilities in accordance with ISO 27005
  • Create recommendations for top management about IT security protection for vulnerabilities

Scale: No Significance (0) · Low Significance (1–2) · Medium Significance (3–4) · High Significance (5). Criteria mark set at 3.
PHASE 3 Threats identification and assessment ▶
Goal: Define list of threats for JOP Corp vulnerabilities and perform assessment procedures.

Threat categories used:
  • Organizational threats
  • Compromise of functions or services
  • Human Actions
  • Technical Failures
  • Infrastructure Failures
  • Natural threats
  • Physical threats

Threat origin: A = Accidental (human actions that can accidentally damage assets) · D = Deliberate (actions aimed at assets) · E = Environmental (not caused by human actions)

Scale: Very Small (1) · Small (2) · Medium (3) · High (4) · Very High (5). Criteria mark set at 3.
PHASE 4 Risks and residual risks identification and assessment ▶
Goal: Define the level of risks and residual risks and perform identification and assessment procedures.

Method: Risk Mark = Likelihood × Business Impact (Severity), calculated using a 5×5 risk matrix.

Likelihood scale: Rare (1) · Unlikely (2) · Possible (3) · Likely (4) · Certain (5)
Business Impact scale: Insignificant (1) · Minor (2) · Moderate (3) · Major (4) · Very Significant (5)

Risk score ranges from 1 (Very Low) to 25 (Very High).
PHASE 5 Controls select and implementation ▶
Goal: Define the implementation and control measurements for defined risks.

Tasks:
  • Perform implementation of risks in accordance with ISO 31000 (27001) and ISO 22301
  • Create a list of recommendations for top management about risks control for IT security protection

Standards applied: ISO/IEC 27001:2013 (Annex A controls) · ISO/IEC 31000:2018 (risk treatment) · ISO/IEC 22301:2012 (business continuity)

Project 2

Boeing IS Policy — Critical Analysis

Boeing's Information Security Policy on Securing Airline Information on Ground and in Air

Course: Information Security Laws and Regulations · ITMO University, 2023 · Supervisor: Livshitz Ilya, PhD

ISO 27001ISO 27002DMARCSupply Chain RiskPolicy Analysis

Boeing's information security policy to protect airline information on the ground and in the air is a critical component of the company's overall security program. This essay analysed that policy against ISO/IEC 27001:2013 and ISO/IEC 27002:2013 — the recognised international frameworks for information security management — identifying alignments, gaps, and specific improvement recommendations.

Policy strengths — ISO alignment
Alignment with ISO 27001 — ISMS scope and risk identification

Boeing's policy aligns with ISO/IEC 27001 by identifying risks to the organisation's information assets and defining the scope of the Information Security Management System. This satisfies the foundational requirements of Clause 4 and provides a structured baseline for all downstream security decisions.

Alignment with ISO 27002 — coverage of core security domains

The policy aligns with ISO/IEC 27002 by providing guidelines and general principles for initiating, implementing, maintaining, and improving information security management. Specifically, the policy covers data classification, access control, incident management, staff training, encryption requirements, and secure communication channels — reflecting the full breadth of ISO 27002 control domains.

24/7 global ethics and incident reporting line

Boeing operates a third-party managed ethics and incident reporting line, staffed 24 hours a day, seven days a week, available across 35 countries. This goes beyond technical controls to embed a security culture at an operational level — demonstrating commitment to people-and-process security.

Critical gaps — identified vulnerabilities and policy contradictions
🔴 Critical — Test/development networks publicly exposed

Despite a stated policy requirement to protect all data with encryption, Boeing's test development networks were publicly exposed to the internet — potentially enabling a sophisticated adversary to gain access to Boeing software source code and build systems. This is a direct contradiction of the policy's own encryption mandate, and represents a critical failure in network segmentation that could result in supply-chain-level compromise affecting downstream aircraft software.

🔴 Critical — Email server infected with malware; no DMARC enforcement

At least one of Boeing's email servers was found infected with multiple strains of malware. Boeing's email domain also lacked DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance) — the protocol that ensures only email from legitimate sources reaches recipients' inboxes. Without DMARC, it would be easy to spoof email coming from boeing.com, enabling targeted phishing campaigns against Boeing employees, third-party suppliers, or government customers who interact with Boeing via email. Given Boeing's defence contracts, this gap has national security implications.

🟠 Medium — No third-party vendor security policy

The ethics line is managed entirely by a third party and staffed around the clock, yet to the best of the analysis, there is no policy outlining how to handle third-party vendors. The policy could provide more guidance on how to handle incidents involving third-party partners or vendors. ISO 27002 §15.1 explicitly requires supplier relationships to be addressed within the ISMS, including contractual security obligations and audit rights.

🟠 Medium — Employee training programme is undefined

While the policy acknowledges the importance of employee training, it could provide more specific guidance on what types of training should be provided, to whom the training must be provided, and how frequently it should be conducted. Without these specifics, compliance with ISO 27002 §6.3 (security awareness, education and training) is immeasurable and essentially unenforceable.

🟠 Medium — Insufficient guidance on secure communication channels

The policy could provide more detailed guidance on how to implement secure communication channels for transmitting sensitive information, particularly in the context of ground-to-air and inter-system data flows specific to the aviation environment.

Structured recommendations
Network Segmentation

Immediately isolate all test/development environments from public internet. Enforce strict DMZ architecture and air-gap critical build systems. Map to ISO 27002 §13.1.3.

DMARC + DKIM + SPF

Deploy the full email authentication stack on boeing.com. Eradicate existing malware infections. Without all three protocols, the domain remains trivially spoofable.

Vendor Risk Programme

Formalise a third-party security policy covering pre-engagement due diligence, contractual obligations, right-to-audit clauses, and vendor incident response procedures. Map to ISO 27002 §15.1.

Training Framework

Define role-based training curricula, delivery modes, minimum frequency (at least annual), and measurable KPIs (completion rates, phishing simulation results). Map to ISO 27002 §6.3.

Secure Comms Guidance

Provide explicit, implementation-level guidance on secure communication channels for sensitive data transmission in ground and airborne contexts.

Overall assessment: Regardless of the possibilities for improvement, Boeing's IS policy does align with international standards and covers a range of critical areas — thereby giving Boeing an edge in protecting itself from possible attacks or incidents. The existing ISO-aligned structure provides a strong platform from which targeted remediation can close the identified gaps.
